Parkinson's Law of Triviality

"The time spent on any item of the agenda will be in inverse proportion to the sum of money involved."
— C. Northcote Parkinson, 1957

What the Law of Triviality Is

Parkinson's Law of Triviality (also known as the "Bike-Shed Effect") observes that groups tend to spend disproportionate time on trivial issues while glossing over important, complex ones. A committee approving a nuclear power plant might spend ten minutes on the reactor design but hours debating what color to paint the bike shed—because everyone can understand and have opinions about paint colors.

The law reveals a fundamental truth about human attention and expertise: we gravitate toward topics we can comprehend and influence, regardless of their actual importance. The result is that significant decisions get rushed through while inconsequential ones consume meeting after meeting.

Why This Happens

Accessibility

Complex issues require expertise to understand. Simple issues are accessible to everyone. People engage with what they understand. A bike shed color is easy to discuss; reactor design requires nuclear engineering knowledge that most committee members lack.

Opportunity to Contribute

People want to participate and be seen contributing. On complex topics, only experts can meaningfully contribute. On trivial topics, everyone can add their two cents. The desire to participate drives attention toward simpler issues.

Avoiding Responsibility

Big decisions carry big consequences. If the reactor fails, someone is responsible. Bike shed color decisions feel safer. Unconsciously, groups may prefer dwelling on low-stakes issues to avoid the anxiety of consequential ones.

False Sense of Productivity

Lengthy debate creates the illusion of diligent work. A meeting that quickly approves major items feels perfunctory. One that debates details extensively feels thorough—even if the debate was about irrelevancies.

The original example (Parkinson, 1957):
Nuclear reactor: approved in 2.5 minutes
Bicycle shed: debated for 45 minutes
Coffee budget: debated for 1.5 hours
Importance and time spent were perfectly inverted

Real-World Examples

Software Development: Teams spend hours choosing a framework or debating code formatting while architectural decisions that will shape the project for years get decided in minutes by whoever happens to be in the room.
Company Strategy: Board meetings devolve into debates about office furniture or catering while major strategic decisions are rushed through without adequate discussion.
Budget Meetings: Committees scrutinize small expense items while approving large budgets with minimal review. Everyone can evaluate whether office supplies are overpriced; few can assess whether a major capital investment makes sense.
Product Design: Teams argue about button colors and font choices while fundamental questions about user needs and product direction receive cursory attention.
Academic Committees: University committees debate course titles and office assignments at length while rubber-stamping curriculum changes that affect thousands of students.

Identifying the Pattern

Signs your group is bike-shedding:
  • Everyone suddenly has strong opinions after being quiet on complex topics
  • Discussion energy increases as issue importance decreases
  • Small details consume most of the meeting time
  • Major decisions are made by default or delegation
  • The least qualified people are most vocal
  • Simple issues keep getting revisited while big ones stay decided

Countering the Law of Triviality

Practical strategies:
  • Time-box topics: Allocate meeting time proportional to importance, not accessibility
  • Big issues first: Tackle complex decisions when energy is highest
  • Delegate trivia: Remove small decisions from committee agendas entirely
  • Require preparation: Distribute materials so people can engage with complex topics
  • Name the pattern: Call out bike-shedding when it happens
  • Expert presentations: Have qualified people frame complex issues accessibly
  • Decision frameworks: Use structured processes for major decisions

For Leaders and Facilitators

Structure Discussions

Don't let discussions flow naturally—they'll flow toward trivia. Actively manage attention. Set time limits. Redirect when discussion veers into bike-shed territory. It's the facilitator's job to keep focus on what matters.

Manage Participation

On complex topics, explicitly invite expert input. On trivial topics, limit discussion or decide by default. Not every topic deserves equal participation—match the discussion format to the importance of the decision.

Create Appropriate Forums

Major decisions might need separate dedicated sessions with relevant experts. Mixing major and minor items in the same meeting guarantees triviality will crowd out importance.

The Deeper Issue

Competence and Engagement

The law of triviality reveals a mismatch between who is in the room and what's being decided. If the people deciding reactor design can't evaluate reactor design, something is wrong with the decision structure. Either get the right people involved, or delegate the decision to those who are qualified.

Democratic vs. Expert Decision-Making

Some decisions benefit from broad input; others require expertise. Treating all decisions as requiring group consensus leads to triviality dominating discussion. Recognize which decisions need democratic input and which need expert judgment.

Using the Law Positively

If you need group buy-in, give people something they can meaningfully engage with. Let them debate the bike shed color while experts handle the reactor. This isn't manipulation—it's recognizing that people want to contribute, and channeling that energy productively.

Parkinson's Law of Triviality isn't about people being stupid—it's about people being human. We engage with what we understand and what feels safe. Effective organizations design structures that account for this tendency, ensuring that importance rather than accessibility determines where attention goes.
