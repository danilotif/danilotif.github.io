Godwin's Law

"As an online discussion grows longer, the probability of a comparison involving Nazis or Hitler approaches one."
— Mike Godwin, 1990

What Godwin's Law Means

Godwin's Law, formulated by attorney Mike Godwin in 1990, observes that as online discussions extend, someone will eventually compare something or someone to Hitler or Nazis. The comparison typically signals the discussion has gone off the rails—emotions have overwhelmed reason, and the original topic has been abandoned for rhetorical escalation.

Godwin created the law deliberately as a "counter-meme" to discourage casual Nazi comparisons, which he felt trivialized the Holocaust and short-circuited substantive debate.

The Underlying Dynamic

Rhetorical Escalation: As arguments heat up, participants reach for more extreme comparisons. Hitler represents the ultimate evil, so invoking him is the rhetorical nuclear option.
Emotional Exhaustion: Long discussions deplete participants' patience and nuance. Crude comparisons replace careful analysis.
Discussion Decay: Productive conversations gradually degrade. The Hitler comparison often marks the point of no return.
Universal Reference: Hitler and Nazis are universally recognized as evil, making them a "default" comparison for anything the speaker opposes.

The Traditional Rule

In internet culture, a common corollary developed: whoever makes the Nazi comparison first has "lost" the argument. The comparison is seen as:

  • An admission of rhetorical defeat
  • A failure of creative argumentation
  • Disrespectful to Holocaust victims through trivialization
  • An attempt to end discussion through shock rather than reason

When Comparisons Are Appropriate

Godwin himself has addressed this nuance. The law was meant to discourage lazy comparisons, not ban all historical analysis. Comparisons may be appropriate when:

Discussing Actual Fascism: When analyzing genuinely fascist movements, historical comparison is legitimate and important.
Holocaust Education: Genuine discussions of genocide and its warning signs require Nazi references.
Specific Parallels: When particular policies or tactics genuinely mirror historical fascism, comparison may be warranted.

Godwin has explicitly "suspended" his law for specific situations where he felt comparisons were genuinely apt, distinguishing between cheap rhetorical moves and legitimate historical analysis.

Implications for Online Discussion

For Participants:

  • Avoid the Comparison: If you're reaching for the Hitler comparison, pause—is there a more precise way to make your point?
  • Recognize the Pattern: When Nazi comparisons appear, the discussion is probably beyond productive
  • Don't Auto-Dismiss: The rule of "whoever mentions Hitler loses" shouldn't prevent legitimate historical analysis
  • Maintain Proportion: Reserve extreme comparisons for genuinely extreme situations

For Moderators:

  • Godwin as Warning: Nazi comparisons often signal a thread has become unproductive
  • Context Matters: Distinguish between rhetorical escalation and substantive historical discussion
  • De-escalation Point: The comparison can be an opportunity to redirect or close a degenerating discussion

Beyond Hitler

The pattern Godwin identified extends beyond Nazi comparisons:

  • Stalin and Mao comparisons serve similar rhetorical functions in some contexts
  • "Orwellian" accusations operate as literary-reference equivalents
  • Any extreme historical reference can function as discussion-ending rhetorical escalation

The underlying principle is that extreme comparisons signal (and accelerate) discussion breakdown.

The Bottom Line

Godwin's Law captures a predictable pattern in online discussions: as they extend and emotions rise, someone will reach for the most extreme comparison available. This usually marks the end of productive exchange and the beginning of rhetorical warfare.

The law serves as both prediction and warning. Recognizing the pattern helps participants catch themselves before making lazy comparisons and helps observers identify discussions that have outlived their usefulness. At the same time, the law shouldn't be used to silence legitimate historical analysis—the goal is thoughtful comparison, not reflexive escalation or reflexive dismissal.
