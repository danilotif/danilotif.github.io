Betteridge's Law of Headlines

"Any headline that ends in a question mark can be answered by the word 'no.'"
— Ian Betteridge, 2009

What Betteridge's Law Means

Betteridge's Law of Headlines, formulated by British journalist Ian Betteridge, observes that when a news headline poses a question, the answer is almost always "no." If the answer were "yes," the publication would state it as fact. Questions are used when writers can't substantiate a claim but want the provocative effect of suggesting it might be true.

The law is a specific instance of a broader pattern: question headlines often signal weak stories. They allow publications to imply sensational claims while maintaining deniability—"We're just asking questions!"

Why It Works

Liability Avoidance: "Is Company X Committing Fraud?" avoids defamation risk. The publication isn't claiming fraud—just wondering about it.
Thin Evidence: When evidence doesn't support an assertion, a question lets you suggest the assertion anyway. "Could Vaccines Cause Autism?" was used when evidence clearly said no.
Engagement Bait: Questions provoke curiosity. "Will This Technology Change Everything?" generates clicks even when the article concludes "probably not."
False Balance: Questions can imply legitimate controversy where none exists. "Is Climate Change Real?" suggests debate among experts who actually agree.

Examples in the Wild

"Is the Internet Destroying Our Brains?" No. But the article explores concerns, cites worriers, and leaves readers anxious despite the evidence.
"Could This Vitamin Cure Cancer?" No. But the publication gets clicks from hopeful readers and hasn't technically made a false claim.
"Will [Politician] Face Charges?" Usually no, or at least not yet. The question lets the outlet imply criminality without evidence.
"Has [Country] Found a Solution to [Problem]?" Almost never in the way the headline implies. The article usually reveals a partial pilot program with limited results.

Why It Matters

  • Misinformation Vector: Question headlines spread false ideas while maintaining deniability
  • Time Wasting: Readers click expecting answers, finding instead "probably not" or "too early to tell"
  • False Controversy: Questions create the impression of debate where scientific consensus exists
  • Manipulation Detection: Recognizing the pattern helps identify low-quality journalism

Using the Law

As a Reader:

  • Apply the Law: When you see a question headline, assume the answer is "no" and assess whether the article is worth your time
  • Check the Conclusion: Scroll to the end—does the article actually answer the question?
  • Notice the Pattern: Publications that rely heavily on question headlines may be low-quality sources
  • Resist Curiosity Bait: The question is designed to hook you; don't bite automatically

As a Writer:

  • Avoid Question Headlines: If you know the answer, state it. If you don't know, should you be writing?
  • Be Direct: Confident assertions serve readers better than coy questions
  • Earn the Claim: If evidence supports an assertion, make it. If not, don't imply it through questions

Exceptions and Nuances

Betteridge's Law isn't absolute:

  • Genuine Uncertainty: Some questions reflect legitimate open questions where the answer truly isn't known
  • Rhetorical Questions: "Isn't It Time We Addressed This Problem?" expects "yes" and is a rhetorical device
  • Opinion Pieces: Columns exploring dilemmas may genuinely ask questions without implying "no"
  • Investigative Teasers: Some question headlines precede articles that reveal the answer is actually "yes"

Still, the law holds often enough to be a useful heuristic for skeptical reading.

The Bottom Line

Betteridge's Law exposes a common journalistic trick: using question headlines to imply claims that can't be substantiated. When publications can prove a sensational claim, they assert it. When they can't, they ask a question—getting the provocative effect while dodging accountability.

For readers, the law is a useful filter. See a question headline? Answer "no" and move on unless you have good reason to engage further. For writers and editors, it's a challenge to resist the easy engagement of question headlines and instead do the work of making warranted assertions or honestly acknowledging uncertainty.
