Say's Law

"Products are paid for with products... A glut can take place only when there are too many means of production applied to one kind of product and not enough to another."
— Jean-Baptiste Say, 1803

What Say's Law Means

Say's Law, attributed to French economist Jean-Baptiste Say, states that supply creates its own demand. In its essence: production generates income, and that income creates demand for other products. There can never be a general oversupply of goods because every act of production creates purchasing power exactly equal to the value produced.

The logic seems compelling: when you produce and sell something, you earn money. That money becomes demand for other goods. If everyone produces, everyone earns, and everyone can buy. The economy is a circle of production and consumption that must balance.

The Classical View

The Circuit Flow: A farmer grows wheat (production), sells it (income), and uses the money to buy tools (demand). The tool-maker's income becomes demand for something else. Production and demand are two sides of the same coin.
No General Glut: Classical economists believed there could be sectoral oversupply (too many shoes, not enough bread) but never general oversupply. Unsold goods in one sector meant unmet demand in another.
Self-Correcting Markets: If supply exceeds demand somewhere, prices fall, making goods attractive. Workers move to sectors with unmet demand. Markets automatically rebalance.

The Keynesian Critique

John Maynard Keynes challenged Say's Law, arguing that demand can fall short of supply:

Hoarding: People can hold money rather than spend it. If income is saved rather than spent, demand falls below production, creating general oversupply.
Investment Uncertainty: Businesses may not invest even with available savings if they're uncertain about future demand. The circular flow breaks.
Sticky Prices and Wages: If prices and wages don't fall quickly enough, markets don't clear, and unemployment results. The "self-correction" can take painfully long.
The Great Depression: Massive unemployment alongside idle factories seemed to disprove Say's Law—supply existed, but demand had collapsed.

When Say's Law Holds

Say's Law works better when:

  • Markets clear quickly: Prices and wages adjust rapidly to balance supply and demand
  • Money is spent: Income flows quickly back into consumption or investment
  • Confidence is stable: Businesses invest and consumers spend predictably
  • Long-run perspective: Over time, markets do tend toward balance

Say's Law fails when:

  • Liquidity preference rises: People prefer holding money to spending it
  • Investment collapses: Uncertainty prevents savings from becoming investment
  • Prices are rigid: Markets don't clear due to sticky prices or wages
  • Financial crises occur: Credit freezes disrupt the flow of spending

Modern Relevance

Supply-Side Economics: Modern supply-siders echo Say's insight—producing more creates more income and ultimately more demand. Tax cuts that stimulate production will generate their own demand.
Demand-Side Economics: Keynesians counter that demand must exist first—production without buyers creates unemployment, not income. Stimulus spending creates demand that induces production.
Entrepreneurship: "Build it and they will come" reflects Say's intuition. New products can create new markets, as Apple demonstrated with the iPhone.
The 2008 Crisis: Collapse in demand without proportionate supply destruction showed demand can fall short, requiring active policy intervention.

The Bottom Line

Say's Law captures an important truth: production does generate income, and income does become demand. In the long run and under normal conditions, supply and demand tend toward balance. But the law's strong form—that demand always equals supply, that general gluts are impossible—has been decisively challenged by economic history.

The debate between Say's Law and its critics underlies fundamental disagreements about economic policy. Should we focus on enabling production (supply-side) or ensuring demand (Keynesian)? The answer depends on conditions: in booms, supply constraints matter most; in recessions, demand constraints dominate. Both perspectives capture partial truths about how economies work.
