Understanding Goodhart's Law

"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."
— Charles Goodhart, 1975

What Goodhart's Law Means

Goodhart's Law states that once a metric is chosen as a target for policy or decision-making, it loses its value as a reliable indicator of the underlying phenomenon it was meant to measure. Originally formulated by British economist Charles Goodhart in the context of monetary policy, this principle has broad applications across business, education, healthcare, and any field where metrics drive behavior.

The law captures a fundamental problem with measurement systems: when people know they're being evaluated on a specific metric, they will optimize for that metric often at the expense of the broader goals the metric was supposed to represent. This gaming of the system can make the metric meaningless or even counterproductive.

How Goodhart's Law Manifests

Educational Testing: When schools are judged by standardized test scores, they start "teaching to the test," narrowing curriculum and neglecting critical thinking skills that aren't measured by the tests.
Call Center Metrics: When customer service is measured by call duration, representatives rush customers off the phone without solving their problems, making calls shorter but service quality worse.
Software Development: When programmers are evaluated by lines of code written, they produce verbose, unnecessarily complex code rather than elegant, efficient solutions.
Police Statistics: When departments are measured by arrest numbers, officers focus on easy, minor violations rather than serious crimes that require more investigation time.

Real-World Examples Across Industries

Healthcare: Hospitals measured on patient discharge speed may release patients too early, leading to readmissions and worse outcomes.
Sales Teams: When measured only on revenue, salespeople may offer excessive discounts or promise unrealistic delivery dates to close deals, hurting long-term profitability and customer relationships.
Social Media: Platforms optimizing for "engagement" (likes, shares, comments) end up promoting divisive, controversial content because it generates more interaction than thoughtful, nuanced posts.
Academic Research: When researchers are judged by number of publications, they split findings into multiple papers rather than producing comprehensive, impactful studies.

Why This Happens

Several psychological and economic factors contribute to Goodhart's Law:

  • Rational Self-Interest: People naturally optimize for how they're being measured and rewarded
  • Unintended Consequences: Simple metrics can't capture the complexity of real-world performance
  • Gaming the System: Smart people find ways to improve metrics without improving actual performance
  • Measurement Distortion: The act of measuring changes the behavior being measured
  • Proxy Problem: Metrics are often proxies for what we actually care about, but proxies can become disconnected from their targets

Recognizing Goodhart's Law in Action

Watch for these warning signs:

  • Metrics improving while actual performance seems to decline
  • People finding unexpected ways to "game" the measurement system
  • Increased focus on activities that boost metrics but don't serve the underlying goal
  • Resistance to changing metrics even when they're clearly being manipulated
  • Emergence of behaviors that technically meet targets but violate the spirit of the goal

Strategies to Mitigate Goodhart's Law

Measurement Design:

  • Multiple Metrics: Use balanced scorecards with several indicators rather than single metrics
  • Leading and Lagging Indicators: Combine predictive measures with outcome measures
  • Qualitative Assessment: Include subjective evaluation alongside quantitative metrics
  • Regular Rotation: Change metrics periodically to prevent gaming
  • Outcome Focus: Measure results rather than activities when possible

System Design:

  • Holistic Evaluation: Consider multiple dimensions of performance
  • Long-term Perspective: Include metrics that capture sustainability and long-term value
  • Stakeholder Input: Get feedback from those affected by the system being measured
  • Gaming Detection: Monitor for unusual patterns that suggest metric manipulation
  • Cultural Emphasis: Reinforce the underlying goals, not just the metrics

Examples of Better Approaches

Customer Service: Instead of just measuring call duration, also track customer satisfaction scores, first-call resolution rates, and follow-up survey results.
Employee Performance: Combine productivity metrics with peer feedback, customer ratings, and contribution to team goals rather than relying solely on individual output measures.
School Assessment: Evaluate schools using multiple indicators including test scores, graduation rates, student engagement surveys, and long-term success tracking.

The Cobra Effect

Goodhart's Law is related to the "Cobra Effect," where well-intentioned policies create perverse incentives. Named after a colonial policy in India that paid for dead cobras, leading people to breed cobras just to kill them for payment.

When Metrics Still Work

Not all metrics fall victim to Goodhart's Law. Metrics tend to remain useful when:

  • The people being measured have strong intrinsic motivation for the underlying goal
  • Gaming the metric would require more effort than actually improving performance
  • The metric directly measures the desired outcome rather than a proxy
  • Multiple stakeholders monitor the metric, making gaming difficult
  • The measurement system is transparent and well-understood

Applications in Modern Organizations

Studies show that organizations using balanced scorecards with 4-6 key metrics tend to perform better than those focusing on single metrics.

The Bottom Line

Goodhart's Law reminds us that measurement is a powerful force that inevitably changes behavior—sometimes in ways we don't intend. The solution isn't to abandon metrics entirely, but to design measurement systems thoughtfully, understanding that people will optimize for whatever we measure.

The key is remembering that metrics are tools to help us achieve goals, not goals in themselves. When we lose sight of the underlying purpose and start treating the metric as the end rather than the means, we fall into Goodhart's trap. Good measurement requires constant vigilance, regular reassessment, and a willingness to evolve our metrics as people and systems adapt to them.
