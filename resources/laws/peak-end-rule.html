Peak-End Rule

"We judge experiences based on how they felt at their most intense point and at the end, rather than the sum or average of every moment"
— Daniel Kahneman, 1993

What the Peak-End Rule Means

The peak-end rule states that people judge an experience largely based on two moments: the peak (most intense point) and the end. The total duration of the experience barely matters—a phenomenon called "duration neglect." Our "remembering self" doesn't average the experience; it samples the extremes.

This means a vacation with one amazing day and a great final day will be remembered more fondly than a vacation with consistently good (but not great) days. The memory of an experience is not the same as the experience itself.

The Classic Experiment

In Kahneman's famous "cold pressor" experiment:

Condition A: Hand in painfully cold water for 60 seconds
Condition B: Hand in painfully cold water for 60 seconds, then 30 more seconds as water warmed slightly (still uncomfortable)

Result: People preferred Condition B, despite experiencing more total discomfort. The improved ending changed their memory of the entire experience.

Real-World Applications

Medical Procedures: Colonoscopies with a brief, less painful period at the end are remembered as less unpleasant than shorter procedures with painful endings—even though the first involves more total discomfort.
Vacations: A mediocre week with an amazing final day is remembered better than a consistently good week with an anticlimactic ending. Save the best for last.
Customer Service: A support call with a frustrating middle but a warm, helpful resolution is remembered better than a call that was okay throughout but ended abruptly.
Presentations: A talk with a powerful climax and memorable closing is judged better than a consistently good talk with a weak ending.

Designing for Peak-End

Create Memorable Peaks:

  • Surprise and delight: Build in unexpected positive moments
  • Achievement celebrations: Make accomplishments feel significant
  • Personalized touches: Create moments that feel unique to the individual
  • Emotional highs: Design for joy, wonder, or meaningful connection

Nail the Ending:

  • Strong closures: End interactions positively, even if there were issues
  • Thank-you moments: Express genuine appreciation at the end
  • Clear next steps: Leave people with clarity and confidence
  • Memorable final impressions: The last interaction shapes the entire memory

Don't Obsess Over Middle:

  • Good enough is enough: The middle matters less than peaks and endings
  • Focus investment: Spend resources on moments that shape memory
  • Accept duration neglect: Longer isn't necessarily remembered as better

Implications for Experience Design

The peak-end rule changes how we should approach experience design:

  • Sequence matters: Put the best at the peak and end, not the beginning
  • Fix endings first: A bad ending ruins a good experience more than a bad middle
  • Create signature moments: Design deliberate peaks rather than uniform quality
  • Duration is overrated: Shorter and memorable beats longer and forgettable
Caution: The peak-end rule describes memory, not in-the-moment experience. The "experiencing self" does feel every moment. Ethical design considers both—don't create miserable experiences with good endings and call it good design.

The Two Selves

Kahneman distinguishes between:

  • The Experiencing Self: Lives in the present, feels every moment
  • The Remembering Self: Creates stories, judges experiences, makes future decisions

The peak-end rule describes how the remembering self works. Since future decisions are based on memories, not experiences, understanding this distinction is crucial for designing experiences that people choose to repeat.

The Bottom Line

The peak-end rule reveals a fundamental truth about human psychology: we don't remember experiences as they actually were. We remember a compressed version dominated by peaks and endings. This isn't a flaw to fix—it's how memory works.

For designers, marketers, and anyone creating experiences: invest disproportionately in the moments that will be remembered. Create genuine peaks. Nail the ending. The memory of your experience is the experience, as far as future behavior is concerned.
